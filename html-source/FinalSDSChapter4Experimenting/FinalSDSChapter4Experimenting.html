Why Prototype?

What is a prototype?

A common question is how is a prototype different from a proof of concept or a pilot? It seems that over different industries, these terms may be used interchangeably. But they do, by definition, have different meanings.

In a simple way, in this process, these three different terms actually form more of a linear process where you start with a proof of concept then move to the prototype and then finally move onto some kind of pilot.

When you have done your research and you know the needs and wants of your customers, you can more easily create a proof of concept - the very first iteration of any solution. The prototype is the next stage of development. It is a physical or digital manifestation of the idea/concept that you created on paper. This Chapter on Experimenting will be primarily looking at using the Proof of Concept and Prototype parts of the process.

Value from prototyping

When you look at the failure rates of new companies, especially the ones in the start-up space, you will notice that there are common reasons why they failed. You will find three common denominators - Time, Money, and Market. Whether it took too long to get to market, they burned through their cash, there was no market need for the “solution”, or there were pricing issues or business model issues, the research says launch fast, fail fast, regroup.

[REASONS FOR FAILURE VISUAL]

Prototyping is a way to test ideas early and more cheaply. Often individuals or teams want to have a nice-looking product or a more complete product ready before they test it in the marketplace but this leads to delays and burning through cash faster.

Delays and perfectionism is even more of a problem in traditional businesses where there is less of an atmosphere of trial and error and a mindset of failing is learning. This old mindset is one of the biggest obstacles to overcome in traditional business settings.

When you test your ideas and concepts early enough, you have not made huge investments into their development yet and this means that your time investment is less, your learning timeline is shortened and you are able to get a tested concept onto the market much faster with fewer problems. This saves time and money. And it also makes sure that the market is ready for what you are offering (because you have already asked and tested it).

Sometimes technologies are just too advanced to be supported out in the market by enough people. You can see that this is what happened with Google Glasses. Huge hype but in the end, the public didn’t want what they had developed. There was never a way to use the technology the way the developers would have liked because the (larger) market didn’t support it. It wasn’t solving a real problem that people had.

Unfortunately, many times, businesses continue down development routes that aren’t looking promising because of the concept of sunk cost. Identified in the late 1970s and early 1980s by Behavioural Scientist Richard Thaler, sunk cost explains why some people make irrational decisions and have “a greater tendency to continue an endeavour once an investment in money, effort, or time has been made”.

How will you use your prototype?

The prototype does not do any good if you keep it in the room or even inside the organisation. A prototype needs to be used with real or potential customers to get any actionable feedback. A prototype helps people see the idea better. It allows them to try the idea, see where the bugs are or it doesn’t make sense, or where the team has blind spots.

In addition to whatever standard questions that you will ask the user (we will go into this more later in the Chapter), you will want to have questions in the back of you mind such as:

  • Is this understandable?
  • Do we need to explain the instructions differently?
  • Do they understand the purpose of what we are trying to achieve

It is only through actual interaction with your prototype that any of these bigger questions can be answered.

Start with low fidelity prototypes to save money, save time, and do the right thing

Building Prototypes

Products Vs Services & Physical Vs Digital

Products vs Services

There are differences between prototyping products and services. One of these differences is related to the ways in which you actually prototype them (this will be explored in the next section). The other main difference is why you are prototyping. For much of product prototyping is to figure out if it will work (viability). But when it comes to services, prototyping comes early because you are testing for:

Value

Experience

Cohesion

﻿[VALUE EXPERIENCE COHESION VISUAL]

Value needs to come first. If you are not designing for value then what are you designing for? Things that have little to no value will have little to no commercial outcomes.

Experience is also an important component of a service prototype because experience will determine whether people will use your service repeatedly. You can find a service valuable but still hate the experience so much that you would rather try something else.  

Cohesion is what will form part of both experience and value. Does it make sense to the customer where it is? Should it be somewhere else in the user journey? We all hate those moments when we realise that if we had only known something earlier, it would have changed our actions in the past/present and then there would be a more successful outcome.

Jon Barnett talks about these in his article where he is referencing Tenny Pinheiro’s The Service Startup.

Prototyping Physical and Digital Concepts

[VISUAL]

Prototypes can be made out of almost anything. Your imagination is the limit. This is, of course, as long as it makes sense to make it from that. When you are creating prototypes, you want to make sure that they are the best use of everyone’s time and effort and that they make sense. This is especially true if you are testing them with customer volunteers.

It is also important to know if you will be testing them first internally or if you immediately test external to your team/organisation. It might be good to test an “alpha” version internally (especially with someone not directly involved in the process) and see how that works. Then do one iteration before going to customers or potential customers. Just to get the most obvious blind spots taken care of.

You can prototype anything. It doesn't matter if it is a service, an app, or a product. All of these are possible to prototype. They just may need to use different methods.

At the end of the previous lesson, we ended with a list of ways you can prototype your product or service whether it is digital or physical. Let's dig deeper into how each of the possibilities actually functions.

Roleplay

When you want to see how a service is provided, acting out the actual service can be a really good first prototype to get fast insight into where the stumbling blocks are. You can do this with your internal team or you could bring in actual users.

[VISUAL]

Source: https://www.servicedesignlab.net/empathy-workshop

Prototyping a service can also be accomplished with a live performance of how it would work. Here you can set up a few scene necessities and even things like signs or thought bubbles, then create a script and act out the service. If you want to show how a service would look when used, or exaggerate how people feel...whatever the reason, this is a legitimate way to create a prototype.

Cardboard recreations/staging

If you want to test out a new floor plan or another physical space or if you want to test different environments, then cardboard recreations are a good way. These can be almost to scale or smaller. You can decide if you want people to actually move through the real space or a representation of the space.

Lego walkthroughs

Lego is another way to visualise and experience services in a physical way. Just as cardboard can be used for larger environments, Lego can be used to create mini versions of environments.

These walkthroughs can be supplemented with paper signs or notes to further explain what is happening. This method is a very early prototyping method. This isn’t really intended to be used with real users necessarily. This can be used to clarify a proof-of-concept within the team.

3D printing

If there are certain components or pieces that need to be tested and the skills exist on your team, after an initial mock up, 3D printing might be a great way to make a testable prototype.

Stuff/proximity prototyping

There is no better way to describe this because these kinds of prototypes are made with the stuff that is close to you. Proximity is key and it really is whatever you have laying around. It might be made out of folded A4 paper or pencils. Many great prototyping materials can be found in craft boxes and/or children’s rooms. Empty containers, chargers, anything.

Paper "digital" mockups

Having a digital product (like an app or saas or even your website) prototyped in paper may seem like it might not be possible, but it is a very quick and cheap way to test very early in the process. This will limit costs and time and provide enough feedback to move onto the next prototype.

In general, these are created using certain software products - MarvelApp, Figma, Sketch, etc. Some are more complex than others. But this process needn’t be only left to specialists. It is also important to keep in mind if you are creating a proof of concept for an internal team or a testable prototype for people external to your organisation to test. One of the most important things to remember is that prototypes come early on in the process. Nobody should be “going to market” with a prototype.

Testing Prototypes & Iteration

Testing Your Prototype

The most important thing you can do with your prototype is to show it to people. The right prototype type should be used to get the most out of talking to your audience. It is important to keep in mind that when you are gathering feedback on your prototype, you want it to be as unbiased and as honest as possible. Here are a few things that you need to keep in mind when testing your prototype:

Avoiding a very polished prototype will generally help people be less shy about giving you negative feedback. If it is too polished, they may feel like you have invested a lot of time and effort into it. This is not what you want. Sometimes less polished will get you better quality feedback.

  • Take a step back from the prototype. This means maybe speaking in more neutral terms about the creation of the prototype (instead of “we have created this prototype/product/service, replace that with “this prototype/product/service has been designed/created…”). People don’t want to hurt your feelings if they think that this is something that you personally have created
  • Asking open questions when wanting to know how easy/difficult they found it
  • Be specific of the function/action you want them to try to complete.
  • You don’t need to have a lot of testing sessions to get the majority of the feedback.

The best way to test a prototype is always with the people who would be using the service but to be honest, that isn’t always possible. If you can’t get a few real users, then second best is to have testers who have no idea about what you are doing. There is no point in having someone who knows more about the service than an average user would. The amount of value that brings is minimal.

[VISUAL]

Preparing for Testing

Low fidelity digital prototypes (either paper mockups or digital mockups) are easy to take to users. Don’t be embarrassed by their lack of a polished look. Research has shown that users are more likely to suggest useful changes to something that they know is still a work in progress. In other words, if it looks too polished, they are less likely to be honest as they see all the hard work that has gone into it and they don’t want to disappoint you.

Just like when you created an interview guide for doing your customer interviews, you can create the same kind of document at the prototyping stage. First you should create the question guide so that the prototype testing is consistent and comparable. Then you should create a form to collect the answers to the questions. Decide what you want to test and decide what outcomes you are looking for.

Who tests it?

There are two sides to testing your prototype. The testers and the users.

The testers should be the people who are involved in creation of the prototype. It might be required that you need someone who is a specialist in digital services but both people should be involved.

The users should be representatives of the actual users and possibly the providers. It needs to make sense and be easy to use for both sides of the service. If it is a physical service, then testing it with both perspectives - the provider and the user - is important but if it is more of a one-sided digital service, then just testing it with the end user, that is ok.

How many tests?

In terms of numbers of tests, you can limit these to 5 or so for each round. There is no need to do an immense amount of testing to get an understanding of most of the issues. This is very similar to the rules that apply when doing in-depth customer research that we looked at in Chapter 2.

How do you find the users who will test your prototype?

This is an interesting question because there are several ways to obtain people to test your prototypes. It is important to understand that you should set these up before your prototype is ready to avoid wasting time. You will need to set a deadline for the prototype to be ready and then make sure that your users have an appointment for a test before that deadline. Another thing that you need to consider is whether you will provide the user with a reward for taking part. These can be vouchers for going to the cinema, gift cards for local stores, or something like an online voucher of some kind.

First, you can ask real company users of your service. Maybe a general email can be sent out asking for those interested in being testers on the next project - include in this email whether there will be some kind of reward for their time and effort. Second, you can put a request in different facebook groups that have your user persona as members (make sure you ask permission from the group owner if it is not you), this also goes for other social media sites. The last one mentioned here is to spend some money on social media advertising. This is useful because you can target who sees the ad which should replicate your persona as much as possible.    

Documenting your tests

Along with the interview guide itself, you should create a form of some kind to record the answers. Maybe you can create a scoring system with linear scales on how easily different actions were performed along with notes. It is probably most important to make sure that the notes from the session are good. This may require that there is more than just the person testing the prototype with the user. A good idea, when possible, is that there is another person there to take accurate notes about how the process went.

A standardised record sheet is great to use if multiple people are conducting the tests. This makes sure that the information that is gathered is understandable by others if for some reason the interviewer cannot present the feedback. It is also good to have this in a common form when gathering the insights from the information and in using those insights to create changes to the prototype.

Using the Feedback

Having created the prototype and conducted the tests, you now have more information about your product/service from actual users. Going back to how we dealt with information earlier in the process, you can stick the important points onto sticky notes and onto a wall. This can be done in two ways, either each person who conducted interviews can do this by themselves in front of the group while telling the story of each user, or the interviewer can tell the story and then the people listening can write sticky notes and put them up when they hear interesting points.

It is also important to collect the data points that you're specifically tested to see how many people found it easy, difficult, or confusing to perform the tasks that you asked them to do. These exercises will help you to identify the insights that came from the interviews. These insights will inform how you iterate your concept further.

Iterating your concept

Using the identified insights, identify the parts of the product or service that you will iterate. Using these insights, you will decide if you will also upgrade the overall look and feel of the prototype for the next round of prototype testing. This may or may not be useful or necessary, you need to decide this.

An example of this upgrade could be seen as going from a paper prototype for the first round to a hybrid or digital prototype for the second round. With more refined processes, it may be worth investing more effort into the look and feel of the prototype as you get closer to the finished product.

[ITERATION VISUAL]

Once you have iterated your prototype, it is probably time to organise a few more tests to make sure that what you did actually fixed the problem(s). This process goes on until you are happy that enough people will be able to use your service or product without much issue. Remember, if many people have issues, this will ramp up your costs in customer service.

Always be ready to follow the evidence- Love the Problem, Not the Solution!

To help people give more unbiased feedback you should:

not show a very polished prototype

tell them what to say

speak in neutral terms

be specific on the actions you want them to perform

ask open questions

tell them how hard you worked on it
