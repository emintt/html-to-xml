Customer Centricity

What is Customer Research?

Customer research is something that is vital to do but that almost everyone says that they don’t need to do because they “already know their customers”. As mentioned in Chapter 1, the delivery gap proved this wrong many years ago. The other issue is, of course, that customers’ needs and wants change with time, technology, and competition. So understanding what the landscape looks like on a regular basis is just good business practice.

Types of data

Quantitative data comes from research that produces numerical data that can be used to give statistics, averages, comparisons, patterns, etc. When companies or news outlets (or anyone) talk about “big data”, they are referring to quantitative data that comes from quantitative research.

Qualitative data comes from research that is generally non-numerical in nature such as interviews, diaries, photos, etc. It is also from the point of view of the individual providing information on their experience and their own context.

Different Phases in Research

Preparatory research

Prep research is all about looking around and making sure that everyone understands the problem. It is about digging a little deeper internally about what everyone feels is going wrong. When using this internally, this can be a very good starting point for a project. Bringing people together - potentially from various functions/units/departments - will help to see where the biases are or where assumptions are being made. Understanding what is happening internally and how the problem is seen, is very important to moving forward and starting the process on a solid foundation. This is because it helps identify early on the different perspectives at play.

This could be done in a workshop format to discuss the problems people see or experience when dealing with this service or product.

Secondary research

Secondary research involves the collection, synthesis, and analysis of existing information. During this research, there will be no research produced by anyone involved in the project. It might be that some people are conducting primary research while others are going through the secondary research, but those doing secondary research will not produce new information.

This research, which is sometimes also called “desk research” (because you should be able to access it from a desk), exists as both qualitative and quantitative research. These are industry reports, trend reports from agencies, journal articles, news articles, blog posts, internal customer data collected from around the organisation, academic research papers, conference abstracts, etc. There are a lot of places to search for data. A good place to start is, of course, your favourite search engine.

Desk research is done first so that a foundation of what information is already available is understood and can be built on, rather than repeated, when primary research is done. Don’t forget to not only take in the information that you find but also to evaluate the level of confidence you have in the information. Evaluate the reliability of the producer of the information as well as the information itself.

It is important and helpful for the rest of the team if summaries of findings can be documented and shared. These are great tools to refer back to when needed - when the process gets stuck or a little off track.

Primary research

Primary research is the research that you conduct yourself (meaning specifically for your project by you or someone on your team). This research means that you will be producing new information through various means. Before we talk about different ways of conducting research, let’s look a little closer at the most common types of primary research that are used in customer research. Here we will look at 3 different types of primary research.

3 Types of Primary Research

Participant approach

This type of research means that the person that you are interviewing is fully aware that they are working together with you as a participant in research.

Self-ethnographic

This type of research is where either the participant or the researcher takes control of the collection of information by self-recording thoughts, feelings, experiences through various means.

Non-participant approach

This type of research is when the person you are collecting information from doesn’t know. This usually involves various kinds of observation.

Knowing these 3 types of research for primary research, helps the person doing research to understand what the options are when faced with research method decisions.

Useful research techniques for each type of research

Participant approach techniques

The most common participant approach techniques are participant observations, in depth, 1-on-1 interviews, focus groups, and surveys.

Participant observation is a method that requires the participant to know that they are being observed. Depending on the focus of the research, what is being observed will change. These can be both digital or physical interactions. This can be watching a person navigate a website (or a new prototype), watching a certain process in their home or their lives (such as their morning routine or the commute to work), etc. So this technique can be the researcher sitting quietly in the corner, it can be the participant narrating what it is they are doing or thinking, it can include shadowing as the participant moves from place to place.

Self-ethnographic techniques

The “self” in self-ethnographic can refer to either the participant or the researcher. This means that the recording of experiences, feelings, accomplishments, and thoughts can be done by either when alone. Your participant can agree to keep a diary (whether written, photographic, digital, etc) and can give that to the researcher to then analyse.

Alternatively, the researcher can conduct an auto-ethnographic piece of research which means that they themselves go through whatever process needs researching and records these same points. There are also certain digital platforms that have been created specifically for self-ethnographic recording.

Non-participant techniques

Non-participant, as mentioned earlier, refers to the fact that the participant does not know that they are participating in research. This can be as simple as counting the number of people that walk past a certain place at a specific time of day (trying to understand the foot traffic of a certain retail location for example). It can be observing where people go outside to smoke and if they are using the designated smoking area outside of a hospital for instance. You could even follow it up with some questions with those people after the observation (adding depth to your research by broadening it with participant research).  

Using non-participant observations can help to confirm or reject what people have self-reported as how they do something. For example, if you interview a bunch of smokers in the hospital and they 100% say that they use the designated smoking area, then you wait and see later what percentage of people really do use it during a set amount of time. You can also use this observation time to see what their body language tells you. In the smoking scenario, are they actively trying to hide or mask their smoking, do they glance at the no smoking sign and purposefully ignore it? Or do they start smoking, see the sign and then move to the right place? All this is useful information.

Assembling the research into insights

Depending on whether this research was gathered by a team or by one person will really determine how you bring this all together. With a team, it will first be necessary to share the information collected and then decipher the insights. The insights may become clearer along the process for someone conducting the research by themselves. Either way, it needs to be shared in an understandable manner with your colleagues.

Analysing the data that you and your team have collected, you can start to see patterns in the kind of information that you uncovered. These patterns will start you on the path to seeing where you might want to dig deeper into your process, product or service for improvement.

Analysing the data through grouping

Affinity diagramming (or grouping or clustering) is when you physically put related or similar information together to see the patterns. Start by putting each of the interesting pieces of information that you identified onto individual sticky notes and put them up on the wall. If there was a team of people doing the research (or parts of the research) have them do the same thing for the research they collected. At the end you should have a wall/window/any flat surface full of sticky notes.

Now, begin to look to see what commonalities they have and pick them up and move those notes near each other. Any duplicates can be placed on top of each other. This grouping will begin to show areas or topics that came up repeatedly. This is where you can start exploring opportunities of improvement (for negative patterns) or see what kind of actions might be replicated in other parts of the customer journey (for positive patterns).

Customer Research Methods

Secondary Research aka Desk Research

It might feel wrong to start with something called “secondary” research but this is the first step in any service development process. This research, as mentioned before, is also called “desk research” and it includes both qualitative and quantitative research elements. Included in desk research are any industry reports, trend reports from trusted sources, journal articles, news articles, blog posts, internal customer data collected from around the organisation, academic research papers, conference abstracts, etc.

Building a Foundation

Desk research is done first so that a foundation of readily available information is understood and can be built on, rather than repeated. Make sure that everyone on the team understands the important insights that have been identified. It is also important to evaluate the level of confidence you have in the information producer when you use these different sources.

Gather these materials through search engine searches, through contacting different units in your company (if you have any) to see what they have, ask around to see if anyone has done any primary research at your organisation that is related to your problem, etc. Read them and see what insights they offer and make sure that you note them down in a way that you can refer to them later if needed.

You will see trends coming up, expectations on how things are progressing in certain sectors, you might even read interesting case studies of recently finished similar projects.

Iterative Process

One thing to remember is that desk research is something that will probably come up again in the process. At this very beginning stage of the process, we are looking for some general information as we have not really defined the real problem yet. And it is important to keep that in mind. Once the customer research has been done and the insights fully gathered and the problem is fully defined, you can then go back to desk research and see how others may have solved a similar problem. This is just more information for your team to add to all the other data you have. This does not mean that you copy them, what is right for their customers may not at all be right for yours.It is also important to look outside of your own industry to find solutions. A good start for learning about this is a book called “Not Invented Here: cross-industry innovation” by Ramon Vullings and Marc Heleven.

PESTLE Analysis

The previous two methods are quite common and widely understood but a PESTLE analysis probably needs a bit more explanation. This acronym stands for Political, Economic, Social, Technology, Legal and Environment and it is a scanning of the entire system around you. You and your team need to look around and see what in each of these areas are affecting you and your company.

PESTLE in More Detail

Political: What legislation or policies are in place or are being discussed that might affect your company or this innovation specifically? What taxation or limitations are in place that might influence how you develop your business?

Economic: Looking at economic risk factors and opportunities such as globalisation, interest rates, consumer spending, etc. Are there any government schemes to promote what you do? Is there any additional investment that you can identify?

Social: Scanning the social and societal side of life will have you look at things like the evolution of consumer tastes, work-life balance issues, population density/diversity, social spending, spending power, basic demographics, etc.

Technology: How is your business incorporating technology into its business development? What is happening in the exponential development of technology in your industry? How are your customers using technology?

Legal: Different sectors have different levels of legal restrictions, what are yours and how might they be changing in the future?  

Environment: How do your customers expect companies to act?  What expectations does your industry and government put on your organisation regarding sustainability and other environmental factors? What is your company mission regarding the environment and how does your problem fit into this?  

You will find some templates for compiling and organising this information online.

If you are running a design sprint or a shorter development process, this can be a less intensive scan where you take a little time to sit together and get everyone to contribute to a quick analysis. Allowing everyone to assess the problem with the same set of knowledge.

Primary Research: Conducting Interviews

Interviewing Skills

It is important to remember that you will probably not just do research once at the beginning but along the process of development, there will be more research that needs to be done. Along the way you will learn what you don’t know or you will discover a nugget of something that will require you to quickly go back a few steps to the research part again. It is important to remember that although the double diamond is drawn as a linear process, it really isn’t.

One-on-one interviewing is one of the most used techniques to gather information from your target group. Interviews will be a resource that you can always do more of if needed, and if you record them properly, you can go back through the data when needed. Conducting this kind of research is vital to the development of any useful service. So much of your future actions will depend on the outcome of what happens right at the beginning of your process. This information, when gathered at the beginning of the process, will provide data that you will return to many times to make sure that you are solving the right problem and designing something of real value for your customers or users.

The first question you may have is “how many do you need to do?”. This, of course, is subjective. There is no one right answer. But it is definitely less than you would use for quantitative data collection. There are a few things to consider when thinking about conducting one-on-one interviews:

Who you interview

Redundancy

Approach

Timing

﻿Who you interview

Make sure that you conduct interviews with the different types of people who will use your service (and maybe even a few that won’t to find out why they won’t for sure). When you are interviewing people, it can also include internal people who would deliver the service or those who currently deliver the service that you are reforming.

You will also consider things such as what their sources of income and access are to certain things. And by this I am just saying that you just need to be sensitive to what their life is like.

Approach

How will this interview be conducted? Will you do an online interview, using something like Zoom, Teams, or Meet, or will it be face-to-face? If face-to-face, where will it be done, at your office, at their place of residence, at the place where the service would usually take place? There are many things to consider when you think about the location. It is good practice in service design to either try to conduct the interview in the place where the service will/does take place or in a place where the interviewee is most comfortable. This level of comfort can allow them to be more open.

Additionally, it is really important for you to consider the situation of the people you will interview. It is not good practice to conduct research in a way that is contrary to the current situation of your potential interviewees. Like asking elderly people to do a Zoom interview (or in other methods, asking them to fill in an online survey) or expecting parents of infants to do interviews in places that are not stroller/pram accessible.

Timing

Is the service that you are designing time-bound? Does it take place at a specific time of day (like a lunch-time yoga class or an in-house daycare)? Or all times of Day (a taxi service)? Ideally, you would be able to conduct the interviews at that specific time to really be able to immerse yourself in their world and to understand what the real needs and fears are. You can also discuss the issues that are currently on their minds at that time of day. It can be harder to conjure up authentic or difficult feelings when you are sitting in a nice cafe in the bright sunshine.

Redundancy

In qualitative research, as face-to-face interviews are, the concept of redundancy is also important in service design. This means that once you start hearing the same information repeated, you have basically reached a saturation point so that interviewing more people becomes somewhat redundant. This is when you know to stop. This may happen after 5 interviews, after 10 or more. This will be more of a determining factor than an arbitrary number.

Creating an interview guide

In order for you to conduct useful and comparable research from your interviews, it is important to create an interview guide. This is a script or plan that you intend to follow. An interview guide is helpful if you have split the interviews between different team members or just to keep you focused and on topic. Interviewing people can be a little difficult at first. When preparing your interview guide, you should give a lot of thought to your questions and potentially make it with others on your team to make sure that they are designed to get the information that you need.

The guide lists all the questions you want to ask (and there will of course be questions that you want to follow on with that aren’t listed). And you may want to add those new questions to any future versions of the interview that you conduct. Adjusting your interview guide is perfectly fine.

Important guidelines for creating a good interview guide:

  • Have no expectations going into the interview - having no expectations means that you are there to listen and learn, not prove your own point.
  • Explain why you are there and why you are conducting the interview - this helps to build trust with the interviewee which is important for authenticity.
  • Ask permission to record if you want to do that - some places require this by law but it is just good practice everywhere and also builds trust.
  • Avoid yes/no questions as much as possible - Sometimes they are necessary but in general, they add little value.
  • Avoid leading questions - these will not get you real answers, just the answers you want. This response is not very valuable.
  • Ask about real experiences - asking them to describe the last time they completed a task will give you insight into how they were feeling and thinking in that moment.
  • Ask follow up questions when appropriate (even if they are not in your guide) - going deeper is never really a bad thing. It helps to create better context and you may want to add these follow up questions to your guide for subsequent interviews.

Read more about interviewing: Portigal, S. (2013). Interviewing users: How to uncover compelling insights. New York: Rosenfeld Media.

Primary Research: Observation and Shadowing

Observing

Observation can be participatory or non-participatory. This means that the person or function that you are observing can know you are there collecting information (participatory) or you can be observing them without their knowledge (non-participatory). This non-participatory kind sounds a little menacing, but it shouldn't. It is basically people-watching...but with a purpose.

If you want to find out if having a "smoking area" somewhere outside a specific building is working as it should (for example at a hospital), a good way to find out how people act when they smoke outside of it is to observe their behaviour. Are they respecting the signs for not smoking in certain areas? Are there even convenient signs at all? Are people looking at them and then ignoring them or are they moving to the designated area after seeing them? How many people do this? You can set a time of 30 mins or 1 hour to observe their actions and record what you see in your notes. Are you noticing that those who seem to be patients (maybe they have hospital gowns on, have a hospital bracelet, or are using a wheelchair or other hospital equipment) obey or not obey the rules any differently than those who do not seem to be patients?

Write your observations in your notes. You can note down how many people in total used the "service". You can go with some predefined things you want to look for.

If you continue the hospital example, you might want to note:

  • How many used the smoking area?
  • Did they seem to be patients or not?
  • Did they seem to see the signage?
  • Did they obey the signs?
  • Were there any people who seemed confused?
  • Were only people who had full mobility able to reach the designated smoking area?

Always make sure you leave room for there to be other, unexpected, observations.

Shadowing

Shadowing is a technique that is similar to observation in that your role is to watch, record, and learn. It, by default, is also a qualitative research method as this is not something that can be done for a great number of people as it is time-consuming. But the depth of possible learning is great. As it is hard to change the way people do something or their behaviour, it can be important to really understand how they do certain things already to try to understand how you can later design your solution with actions that people are already comfortable taking.

Just like interviews, shadowing requires you to prepare in advance. It is important to know what it is that you want to observe. Do you want to see how this person gets ready for work in the morning? Do you want to learn how they use a certain search engine or how they pick up their mail? Do you want to watch them order something online?  Knowing what you want to observe and how you will observe them is vital to actually learning. It is also important to be able to accurately record what it is that is happening. You may need to prepare some kind of sheet where you can jot down predetermined instructions and how they were actually navigated. It might be a really good idea to use video to record the important interactions so that you aren't trying to remember what happened at some point in the future. For a more in-depth description of shadowing, you can check out the Interaction Design website.  

You can observe this difficulty whenever a software changes its interface and people struggle to figure out where to find the commands or buttons they are looking for. Many people got so used to the way the Start Menu works in Microsoft Office products that there was a huge problem when they re-designed it to look and work differently. To the point where they brought it back. Trying to change behaviour can be a real problem for companies which can lead to an underestimation in the resistance and the success of further innovations.

Building Personas

What Is A Persona?

A persona is a fictional representation of a group of people. It is not a specific person you know, nor is it based on one real person, it is based on the entire group of who you are trying to represent. It is normal that a company or organisation would have more than one persona created because their company or organisation would normally have more than one type of user or customer. A persona is needed for each of the groups that you serve.

Personas, rather than representing a specific demographic (as is used in other industries in the past (and present), are representations of certain behaviours or drivers/motivators your customers have. Traditional separators such as age, income, gender, postal code, etc are becoming obsolete for many customer personas. This is because, as services and products become more widespread and personalised, these differentiators become much less important and indicative of people’s actions and intentions. Creating personas based on what motivates them can end up being much more meaningful in terms of outcomes. For both the customer and the company.

Personas must be created using the data and insights that you have collected during your research phase. At no point should you "guess" to create your persona(s).

The point of creating personas is to create empathy within the team to incentivise them to create better products, services and/or processes. One of the benefits of doing customer research at the beginning of any project is that it gives you access to the thoughts and feelings of so many people relevant to your business. It is with this data that you should begin to build your personas. You should be careful not to let assumptions and stereotypes creep into your personas.

If done correctly, and some demographic information is used in their creation (but not based on it), these personas can be more easily remembered and considered when it comes time to use them in relation to business cases.

How to define your personas

In a workshop setting, you can co-create your personas with the rest of your team. A good time to do this is either at the same session where you present your research data or very soon afterwards. While talking about the people that you collected data on or with, you can begin to note down different aspects of the time you spent with/near them. How was their body language, what kinds of words did they say, what kind of quotes did they use and you noted, what did they say were their challenges or their motivations? There are so many ways to pull information about these people from the data you collected.

Once all of this personal information has been noted down on to sticky notes, it is now time to do another grouping exercise. This time you are looking for behaviours and drivers/motivators that are in common.

A really great example of how to group people by their motivations is what Ben Ralph did in his 2017 article called Creating Personas. He uses a simple example that is about a scenario for a bank. A bank has many, many different personas. So in the bank’s case it would be useful to break it down based on the service that they want to use. This means that overall, there will be a lot of different personas but for each service there will just be 5 or 6 maybe. For the mortgage department, there are many people who want to buy homes. But that is too broad of a definition when it comes to who their customers are. Their personas will define the tone, the words, and the visuals that they use in their marketing. It also will help them decide who the customers are that they want to address and those that they may wish to not consider. This is why knowing your customers really well is important.

Once the grouping has been done, your job is to identify a ‘red thread’ that unifies the people. This will help you to identify the axes for your “persona matrix”.

A persona matrix is a graph where you can plot two different motivators against each other. The labels that go on the matrix will be specific to the people that you are plotting.

For example, designing services for refugees might require comparing their education level and their language proficiency.

Designing a product for travellers might require you to compare budget and risk level.

For the mortgage department at the bank, this can be represented by how quickly they want to purchase a house and how much money they have access to.

Once you have identified the axes that you want to use (and this can take some testing and trying on different labels), then you can begin to start plotting the groups that you identified. See the graphic to see the outcome of this:

Components of a Persona

Once you have decided what possible personas exist in your space, and which ones that your company will pursue as customers, it is time to create an individual representative persona for each group.

You can do this by filling in a persona template. There are many persona templates out there, Google will show you them if you just look. You can also use the one that has been created for this course. A persona is a one page brief that is a representative, fictional person. This should include a name, an age, a profession, possibly a life situation (like parent, caring for elderly parents, employed, entrepreneur, etc), and a quote that is representative of their experience.

It is also important information about their goals, challenges, motivations, values, needs, interests, and preferences. In the provided Persona Template there are prompts in each section so that you know what you should include.

Using a persona

The value of a persona

  • You can see problems and goals of customers very quickly
  • It gets different colleagues on the same page quickly as to who they serve and what they want
  • It helps to embed empathy as a starting point in any product/service development through deep customer understanding
  • It can help you identify who you want and who you don’t want as customers (sometimes certain groups aren’t quite ready for your solution or helping get them there would be too costly)
  • It helps you to identify the words, phrases, and tone needed to speak to your customers via marketing, sales, and support.

But one of the most important uses for a persona is for keeping the customer at the centre of your decision making.

When you have this person relying on you to help them reach their goals and to help lessen their frustrations and pain points, and you keep coming back to them during the process, your product or service will get better.

During internal discussions, your persona(s) should have a seat at the table, referring to them by name when discussing features and processes.

Building a Customer Journey

What is a customer journey map?

Customer journey maps are not what one person in the team thinks happens or even what a team of "experts" thinks happens. A customer journey map is built entirely from the experience and perspective of the customer.

Journey mapping is a process where you combine two basic human communication tools - storytelling and visualisation. Both storytelling and visualisation help people understand certain concepts better. They also help to build empathy. This kind of mapping is a way of seeing your service from the user’s perspective. It allows you to use your persona to walk through their journey to gain insights into pain points (where your service is not living up to its promise) and to what is going right. It is also a way to see a bigger picture than just the part of the experience that your everyday day work may hide.

Journey maps also visualise the experience over time. The experience that a user or customer has is not a single moment event. You can see that with return policies. If it was a single moment event, you would buy the product or service and that would be it. But everyone understands that it takes time for an experience to unfold.

In the eyes of the user, your company or organisation is just one service provider (even if you outsource various functions or collaborate with other companies). In the mind of the user, a service, no matter who is responsible for it in the back end, expects it to be connected and seamless.

The most important thing to remember about a Customer Journey Map is that it is based on the customer research that you do.

Components of a basic customer journey map

If you search online for a journey map, you will find many different variations. It can be daunting. But when creating a simple customer journey map, there are just five rows (or lanes as some call them) of information.

We have already talked about the very top row of the journey map: the point in the timeline of the map. But what other lanes are on a map? For a basic functional map that will teach you something, it is important to have at least these 4 rows of information:

Customer actions

are defined as what the customer is actually doing at that moment in the sequence. Describing their actions.

Customer goals

are all about what the customer wants to accomplish. They might be searching the internet for a plumber (action) but what their goal is to get the water to stop spraying in their bathroom (goal).

Touchpoints (or channels)

are the ways in which the customer is interacting with your company. What are the methods that they are communicating with you? Email, phone, in-store, website, etc.

Experience

is the emotional experience that they are having in that action while trying to fulfil that goal. In the example, we can see that when Sara wants to hear from the company but they are not getting back to her, her emotional level is very low (probably frustrations and irritation). What kind of map is needed?

There are 2 decisions that need to be made when starting to create a journey map:

Time

Level of Detail

﻿The time perspective means whether you are creating a current status or desired future map

AND

The level of detail you will use - broad overview level or specific detail level﻿

You can do a high level analysis where you go through a very broad overview of your service within the organisation right down to a level of analysis that goes through the login process. You need to define what you are mapping.

Current Status and/or Future Desired State

In the first instance, time, we mentioned that there are 2 time perspectives. It can be a map reflecting how things are currently done or it can be a map of a desired future. Often it is good to have both so that everyone truly understands the starting point. One hindrance to improving a service (or product) experience is not knowing how the experience is currently going, and therefore, not knowing where to go.

The desired future journey map will help you to identify where your improvements need to be targeted so that your desired user experience will be met. It is the same as picking an outcome or a destination and working your way back to see what needs to happen (and change) between now and the end date for your goal to be achieved.

Levels of Detail

The second decision that has to be made is the level of detail you are going to use to create your journey map. The highest level of journey map is where you map the Before, During, and After actions in a broad manner on a single map. As you will see from the illustrated maps below, each subsequent level of map digs deeper into a certain section of your customer’s experience.  

[LEVELS OF DETAIL VISUAL]

In this Journey Map Detail Levels example, you can see how each next level digs deeper into the previous one. As a team, you need to decide which level of detail you wish to look at. If you want to journey map what the decision process is to get to the point where a customer will buy your product/service, then you are looking at a level 2. If you want to map how they start using your service, that is probably a level 3. If you want to look in minute detail at the email flow (the number or messages/what the messages say/how instructions are given, etc.) at the point of when a person first logs into your service, that is a level 4.

A journey map can be thought of just like a geographical map, which, depending on how zoomed in you are, will show you a place that will take you 24 hours to get to by plane, 5 hours to get to in a car, or 15 minutes by walking. Your detail level will also determine the time length of the experience. Is it, for example, a whole week at level one, a day or two at level 2, an hour at level 3 or, just a few minutes at level 4?

So agree on the level and the perspective of the map and you can begin to look at filling a map of your own in.

Using a customer journey map

The beginning of this process started with a lot of research. This means that your persona is based on research and the customer journey map that you create can also be based on research. It is so important to make sure that the journey is based on research and not guessing because this is where you will begin to look at where and how you can make the experience of your customer better. If this is done on an assumption based map, then you are essentially only guessing. And basing changes on how you do something or how you produce something on a guess, is not going to be a great strategy in the short or long term.

As mentioned, the journey map should be a journey that represents a single persona that you have created. Each persona type will have a different journey. It is also a good idea to create these so that you can understand how their journeys are different. This will show you where you need to supplement different parts of your experience or where you need to make new paths or possibilities for them.

The persona and the journey map are intertwined in a way that will help guide your company to a greater understanding of who your customers are, what they need, and how they use your products and services. They also help with the empathy needed to really create value for them and to want to create real value for them.

A research-based, persona-led journey map, it must be strongly emphasised, is the tool needed to begin to redesign your experience (at whatever level is needed). It is neither an outcome nor a piece of art (although it will feel like a piece of art with all the hard work that goes into it). As contradictory as it is, a journey map is both a snapshot in time and something that is always changing. As Mark Stickdorn, who is, among other things, a co-author of two foundational service design books: This is Service Design Thinking (2012) and This is Service Design Doing (2018), says "Customer Journey Maps are not a deliverable!". This means that the point of the map is not to have the map. It is to use the map as a starting point for creating your deliverables (improved services, new services, better customer experiences, etc.). A customer journey map is where the hard work begins to design better services because now you know where there are some pain points.

Creating a Journey Map

Physically creating a journey map can be as simple as taking a blank piece of paper and drawing the different necessary sections on it. It needn't be pretty or complicated. It just needs to be understandable by those who will look at it. A great way to create a first version of a customer journey map is a large piece of paper (maybe from a flip chart or a roll of paper) and sticky notes. The sticky notes are important because they are movable. Nobody ever gets a journey map right in the first attempt. The ability to move steps in the process to the left and right is essential as more information becomes clear. You may have a cross-unit group that reminds people of forgotten steps or misunderstood processes. This is normal and expected. By starting in this agile and temporary way, you allow more information to be added or removed as needed.
